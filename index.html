How this solar calculator works

Most solar calculators online still assume the 30% federal tax credit. That credit — the residential Clean Energy Credit (Section 25D) — expired for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025 (here's exactly what changed and what still counts). This calculator is built for the 2026 reality, so the numbers you see reflect what cash and loan buyers actually pay today.

Here's the math, with nothing hidden:

  • Production = system size (kW) × your state's peak sun hours × 365 days × 0.83 system-loss derate × your shading factor.
  • System size is set to offset roughly 100% of your annual electricity use.
  • Gross cost = size × $/watt (national average ≈ $2.85/W, editable).
  • Net cost = gross cost − federal credit (now $0 for cash/loan in 2026) − any state/local incentives you enter.
  • Annual savings = energy offset valued at your electricity rate, then grown 3%/yr for rising utility prices and reduced 0.5%/yr for panel aging.
  • Payback period = the year your cumulative savings finally exceed your net cost.

Everything above is editable in the “Advanced assumptions” panel. If you don't trust an input, change it — that's the point.

Is solar worth it in 2026?

Short answer: it depends far more on where you live than it used to. With the federal credit gone for purchases, the two levers that decide whether solar pays off are your electricity rate and your sun:

  • Strong case: high-rate, sunny states like California, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Arizona and Nevada — payback can still land in a reasonable window.
  • Weaker case: low-rate states (Washington, Utah, North Dakota, Louisiana) where cheap power means solar takes much longer to pay back.
  • Lease / PPA: with $0 down, the third-party owner may still claim a commercial credit through 2027, so this path can offer day-one savings without the long payback — but you don't own the asset or capture its full value.

Is solar worth it in 2026 without the federal tax credit?

The 30% residential credit (25D) expired for systems placed in service after Dec 31, 2025. Solar can still pay off in high-rate, sunny states, but cash/loan payback periods are now longer. What's a good solar payback period?

Many homeowners aim for under ~10–12 years so the system pays for itself well within its 25+ year life. After the federal credit's expiry, watch this number closely in low-rate states.
